**The Glasshouse Bell Case: Comprehensive Investigation Report**

At 7:18 p.m. on 14 March, during a private preview at the Halcyon Botanical Archive, conservator Mara Venn was found dead inside the sealed orchid vault, a climate-controlled glasshouse used to preserve rare night-blooming specimens. The vault door had been locked from the inside, the emergency latch had not been broken, and the only roof vents were too narrow for a person. A silver service bell lay beside her hand, though no one admitted hearing it ring.

The death initially appeared impossible. Six people were present in the archive: director Oren Vale, donor Livia Saye, security chief Tomas Rook, botanist Elian Greer, caterer Nessa Holt, and apprentice archivist Jun Park. The culprit was Elian Greer.

The motive was not jealousy, inheritance, or panic. Greer had been secretly selling sterile cuttings from the archive’s protected collection. Mara discovered the theft that afternoon after noticing falsified propagation tags and planned to expose him after the donor preview. Greer knew she would inspect the orchid vault alone before the night-bloom display, so he prepared a murder that depended on everyone misunderstanding when the fatal act occurred.

At 5:40 p.m., Mara confronted Greer in the misting corridor. Jun saw them arguing but heard only the words “not after tonight.” Jun assumed they meant funding, missing the threat. At 6:05, Nessa delivered trays to the west gallery and noticed Greer wiping soil from his cuff; she thought nothing of it because he often handled plants. At 6:22, Tomas tested the security shutters and logged all external doors closed. At 6:31, Livia and Oren entered the lecture room together, where both remained visible to guests. At 6:46, Mara entered the orchid vault. Tomas saw her on camera and saw the vault door close. He did not see Greer enter because Greer had already hidden inside the adjacent service alcove before the camera angle covered the corridor.

The key fact is that the vault was not truly locked from the inside when Mara entered. Greer had earlier wedged the automatic strike plate with a sliver of transparent grafting plastic. After Mara stepped in, the door appeared closed but had not fully latched. Greer entered two minutes later through the service side, struck her with a brass humidity weight, and injected concentrated potassium chloride through a puncture hidden beneath a pruning scratch on her wrist. He then staged a struggle, placed the bell near her hand, removed the plastic wedge, and left by pulling the door shut with a length of green binding wire threaded through the latch gap. Once the wire was withdrawn, the door finally locked, creating the illusion that Mara had been sealed in before the attack.

The timeline is complete. At 6:50, Greer killed Mara. At 6:56, he exited through the service alcove. At 7:02, he appeared in the fern gallery, where Nessa saw him speaking calmly to a guest. At 7:09, Jun noticed condensation inside the vault glass and thought Mara was adjusting humidity. At 7:14, Tomas saw no movement on the vault camera but assumed Mara was kneeling behind the central specimen bench. At 7:18, Oren opened the public display sequence, the vault alarm failed to acknowledge Mara’s manual confirmation, and Tomas forced entry with the director’s override.

The evidence resolves the case. The bell had no fingerprints from Mara’s right hand, though she was right-handed; Greer placed it. The brass humidity weight had been cleaned, but its calibration groove contained a trace of Mara’s blood. A strand of green binding wire was later found caught behind the strike plate. The grafting plastic sliver was discovered in Greer’s apron pocket, cut to the exact width of the vault latch. The orchid mist logs showed a door-humidity disturbance at 6:48, after Mara had supposedly locked herself in. Mara’s notebook contained the partial phrase “E.G. tags reversed,” proving she had identified Greer’s falsification. Finally, Greer’s claim that he never entered the orchid wing after 6:30 was contradicted by pollen from a night-blooming Angraecum found on the sole of his left shoe.

Each witness held only part of the truth. Jun knew Mara and Greer argued but missed its meaning. Nessa saw Greer dirty and later composed, but not the transition. Tomas trusted the camera and therefore missed the alcove blind spot. Oren knew Mara was about to give him a confidential report but did not know its subject. Livia knew Greer had pressed her for private access to rare specimens, but she mistook it for academic ambition. Greer alone knew that the door could appear closed without being locked.

The information asymmetry made the case solvable. No witness lied materially except Greer, but each witness interpreted observations through ordinary assumptions: locked doors are locked, bells are rung by victims, cameras show entrances, and botanists carry plant residue. The correct reasoning requires rejecting the apparent locked-room premise and asking not how the killer escaped a sealed room, but when the room became sealed.

The final reconstruction is therefore as follows: Greer disabled the latch before Mara entered, waited unseen, murdered her inside the vault, staged evidence of a later attack, withdrew the latch wire from outside, and rejoined the preview before discovery. The conclusion is high confidence. The physical evidence, motive, false alibi, and timing all converge on Greer, while every other suspect lacks both the hidden access knowledge and the opportunity window.
